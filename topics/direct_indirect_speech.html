Direct and Indirect Speech - Master the Art of Reporting

Direct and Indirect Speech

When we communicate what someone else has said, we can use either direct speech or indirect speech (also called reported speech). Understanding the rules and differences between these two forms is essential for clear and accurate communication in English.

What is Direct Speech?

Direct speech repeats the exact words spoken by someone, using quotation marks. It presents the speaker's words exactly as they were spoken, preserving the original tone and immediacy of the statement.

Example: Sarah said, "I am going to the store."

What is Indirect Speech?

Indirect speech (or reported speech) reports what someone said without using their exact words and without quotation marks. It involves changes in pronouns, verb tenses, and time or place references to accurately reflect the reporting context.

Example: Sarah said (that) she was going to the store.

Key Differences

Direct Speech

  • Uses quotation marks to enclose the exact words
  • Reports exact words as originally spoken
  • Preserves the original tense used by the speaker
  • Maintains original pronouns (I, you, we, etc.)
  • Keeps original time and place references (now, here, this, etc.)
  • Preserves the emotion and immediacy of the original statement

Indirect Speech

  • No quotation marks are used
  • Paraphrases or summarizes what was said
  • Generally shifts verb tenses backward in time
  • Changes pronouns according to the reporting context
  • Adjusts time and place references (then, there, that, etc.)
  • Often includes a reporting clause with "that" (which can be omitted)

Rules for Converting Direct to Indirect Speech

1. Pronoun Changes

When converting from direct to indirect speech, pronouns often need to change based on the context and the reporter's perspective.

Rule: Pronouns change according to the context of the reporting and the relationship between the speaker and the listener.

Direct Speech Indirect Speech
"I am happy," he said. He said (that) he was happy.
"You are late," she told me. She told me (that) I was late.
"We will help you," they promised. They promised (that) they would help us/me.
"They have finished their work," he said. He said (that) they had finished their work.

2. Tense Changes

One of the most important aspects of indirect speech is the backshift of tenses. When the reporting verb (said, told, asked, etc.) is in the past tense, the verb in the reported speech typically moves one tense back in time.

Rule: When the reporting verb is in the past tense, shift the tense of the reported verb backward in time.

Direct Speech Indirect Speech Tense Change
Present Simple: "I work here." He said (that) he worked there. Present Simple ‚Üí Past Simple
Present Continuous: "I am studying." She said (that) she was studying. Present Continuous ‚Üí Past Continuous
Present Perfect: "I have finished." He said (that) he had finished. Present Perfect ‚Üí Past Perfect
Past Simple: "I visited Paris." She said (that) she had visited Paris. Past Simple ‚Üí Past Perfect
Future (will): "I will help." He said (that) he would help. Future (will) ‚Üí Conditional (would)

Note: If the direct speech contains a past perfect tense, it remains the same in indirect speech.

Example: "I had already eaten," she said. ‚Üí She said (that) she had already eaten.

Exception: If the reported content is still true at the time of reporting or is a universal truth, the tense may remain unchanged.

Example: "The Earth revolves around the Sun," the teacher said. ‚Üí The teacher said (that) the Earth revolves around the Sun.

3. Time and Place Reference Changes

Words that refer to time and place often need to change in indirect speech to reflect the different context of reporting.

Rule: Adjust time and place references to reflect the perspective of the reporter at the time of reporting.

Direct Speech Indirect Speech
today that day
yesterday the day before / the previous day
tomorrow the next day / the following day
now then
here there
this that
these those
ago before
last week the week before / the previous week
next week the following week / the week after
Direct: "I will see you tomorrow," he said.
Indirect: He said (that) he would see me the next day.

4. Modal Verb Changes

Modal verbs follow specific patterns when changing from direct to indirect speech, typically shifting to their past forms.

Direct Speech Indirect Speech
can could
may might
will would
shall should/would
must had to / must (obligation)
Direct: "I can swim," she said.
Indirect: She said (that) she could swim.

Note: 'Could', 'might', 'should', 'would', 'ought to', and 'used to' remain the same in indirect speech as they are already past forms.

5. Reporting Questions

When reporting questions, we use verbs like 'asked', 'inquired', 'wanted to know', etc., instead of 'said'. The word order changes to that of a statement, and question marks are removed.

Rule for Yes/No Questions: Use 'if' or 'whether' to introduce the reported question.

Direct: "Do you like coffee?" he asked.
Indirect: He asked if/whether I liked coffee.

Rule for Wh-Questions: Keep the question word (what, where, when, etc.) and change word order to statement form.

Direct: "Where do you live?" she asked.
Indirect: She asked where I lived.

6. Reporting Commands and Requests

Commands and requests are reported using the infinitive form. The reporting verbs commonly used are 'told', 'ordered', 'commanded', 'requested', 'asked', etc.

Rule: Use 'to + infinitive' for positive commands and 'not to + infinitive' for negative commands.

Direct (Positive Command): "Open the window," he said.
Indirect: He told me to open the window.
Direct (Negative Command): "Don't be late," she said.
Indirect: She told me not to be late.
Direct (Request): "Please help me with this," he said.
Indirect: He asked/requested me to help him with that.

7. Reporting Suggestions and Offers

Suggestions and offers are typically reported using different reporting verbs like 'suggested', 'offered', 'proposed', etc.

Direct (Suggestion): "Let's go for a walk," she said.
Indirect: She suggested going for a walk.
Direct (Offer): "Can I help you?" he said.
Indirect: He offered to help me.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to change pronouns appropriately based on the context
  • Not shifting tenses when reporting past events (backshift rule)
  • Neglecting to change time and place references
  • Keeping question word order in reported questions
  • Using quotation marks in indirect speech
  • Using 'said to' instead of 'told' when mentioning the listener
  • Forgetting that 'say' is used without an indirect object, while 'tell' requires one

Pro Tip: When in doubt about whether to use backshift in tenses, consider whether the reported statement is still true or relevant at the time of reporting. If it expresses a general truth or a continuing situation, the backshift may be optional.
