Fixing Common Errors, One Word at a Time!

Common English Errors Made Simple

Everyone makes mistakes! Even native English speakers get confused by the tricky rules of the language. On this page, we'll explore some of the most common errors people make when speaking or writing English, and learn how to fix them. Ready to become a grammar detective? Let's spot those mistakes together!

üî§ Confusing Word Pairs

Their, There, They're

I left my book over their. I left my book over there.

Their shows possession (their book). There refers to a place. They're is short for "they are."

Your vs. You're

Your going to love this game! You're going to love this game!

Your shows possession (your toy). You're is short for "you are."

Its vs. It's

The dog wagged it's tail. The dog wagged its tail.

Its shows possession (belonging to it). It's is short for "it is" or "it has."

Affect vs. Effect

The medicine had a positive affect. The medicine had a positive effect.

Affect is usually a verb (to influence). Effect is usually a noun (a result).

To, Too, Two

I want to go to the movies to. I want to go to the movies too.

To is a preposition or part of an infinitive. Too means "also" or "excessively." Two is the number 2.

Weather vs. Whether

I don't know weather I should go outside. I don't know whether I should go outside.

Weather refers to atmospheric conditions. Whether introduces alternatives or possibilities.

üìù Subject-Verb Agreement

Singular Subjects

The dog with all the toys were excited. The dog with all the toys was excited.

The subject is "dog" (singular), so use "was" (singular verb), not "were."

Everyone/Everybody

Everyone are ready for the party. Everyone is ready for the party.

"Everyone" is singular and needs a singular verb ("is"), even though it refers to multiple people.

Either/Neither

Neither of the cats are eating. Neither of the cats is eating.

"Neither" is singular and takes a singular verb ("is"), even when followed by a plural.

Collective Nouns

The team are playing well today. The team is playing well today.

In American English, collective nouns like "team" are usually treated as singular.

Compound Subjects with "and"

Jack and Jill is going up the hill. Jack and Jill are going up the hill.

When two subjects are joined by "and," use a plural verb ("are"), unless they form a single unit.

Subjects with "or" or "nor"

Either the dog or the cats is making that noise. Either the dog or the cats are making that noise.

When subjects are joined by "or/nor," the verb agrees with the subject closest to it.

‚ùó Punctuation Problems

Comma Splices

I love chocolate, it's my favorite dessert. I love chocolate. It's my favorite dessert.

Don't join two complete sentences with just a comma. Use a period, semicolon, or add a conjunction.

Apostrophe Errors

The book's were on the shelf. The books were on the shelf.

Don't use apostrophes to make words plural. Use apostrophes for possession or contractions.

Missing Commas in Series

I need to buy apples oranges and bananas. I need to buy apples, oranges, and bananas.

Use commas to separate items in a series. The final comma (Oxford comma) is often recommended for clarity.

Quotation Marks

She said "I'm hungry". She said, "I'm hungry."

In American English, periods and commas go inside quotation marks. Add a comma before quoted speech.

Run-on Sentences

I ran to the store I needed some milk. I ran to the store. I needed some milk.

Don't run two complete sentences together without proper punctuation. Use a period, semicolon, or conjunction.

Colon vs. Semicolon

I have one goal in life; to be happy. I have one goal in life: to be happy.

Use a colon to introduce examples or explanations. Use a semicolon to join related independent clauses.

üåü Misused Words

Accept vs. Except

I will except your gift. I will accept your gift.

Accept means to receive. Except means to exclude.

Fewer vs. Less

There are less cookies in the jar now. There are fewer cookies in the jar now.

Use fewer for countable items (cookies). Use less for uncountable things (sugar, time).

Loose vs. Lose

Don't loose your keys again! Don't lose your keys again!

Loose means not tight. Lose means to misplace something or not win.

Then vs. Than

She is taller then her brother. She is taller than her brother.

Then relates to time. Than is used for comparisons.

Lie vs. Lay

I'm going to lay down for a nap. I'm going to lie down for a nap.

Lie means to recline (no object). Lay means to put something down (needs an object).

Who vs. Whom

Who did you give the present to? Whom did you give the present to?

Use who for the subject of a verb. Use whom for the object of a verb or preposition.

‚è±Ô∏è Verb Tense Errors

Inconsistent Tenses

Yesterday I go to the store and bought some candy. Yesterday I went to the store and bought some candy.

Keep your tenses consistent. If you start in the past tense, stay in the past tense.

Present Perfect vs. Simple Past

I never seen that movie before. I have never seen that movie before.

Use present perfect (have/has + past participle) for experiences with no specific time.

Conditional Sentences

If I would have known, I would came earlier. If I had known, I would have come earlier.

For past conditionals, use "if + had + past participle" and "would have + past participle."

Irregular Verbs

She teached me how to swim. She taught me how to swim.

Many common verbs have irregular past forms that must be memorized (teach ‚Üí taught).

Past Participles

I have wrote a letter to my friend. I have written a letter to my friend.

With "have/has/had," use the past participle form of the verb (written, not wrote).

Would of / Could of

I would of helped if I had known. I would have helped if I had known.

"Would of" is incorrect. The proper form is "would have" (often contracted to "would've").
