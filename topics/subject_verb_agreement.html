Subject-Verb Agreement

Subject-verb agreement is a fundamental grammar rule stating that the verb in a sentence must agree in number (singular or plural) with its subject. This ensures clear and grammatically correct sentences.

üìùBasic Agreement

  • Rule: Singular subjects take singular verbs, and plural subjects take plural verbs.
    • Singular: The cat sits on the mat.
    • Plural: The cats sit on the mat.

üîÑCompound Subjects

  • Rule 1 (and): When two or more subjects are joined by "and," they generally take a plural verb.
    • John and Mary are going to the party.
    • The book and the pen were on the table.
  • Rule 2 (or/nor): When two or more subjects are joined by "or" or "nor," the verb agrees with the subject closer to it.
    • Neither the students nor the teacher was present.
    • Neither the teacher nor the students were present.
    • Either the dog or the cats are making a mess.
    • Either the cats or the dog is sleeping.

üôãIndefinite Pronouns

  • Rule: Some indefinite pronouns are always singular, some are always plural, and some can be either depending on the context.
  • Always Singular: each, every, everyone, everybody, someone, somebody, anyone, anybody, no one, nobody, either, neither.
    • Each student has a textbook.
    • Everyone is excited about the trip.
    • Neither of the options is suitable.
  • Always Plural: several, few, both, many.
    • Several students were absent.
    • Both of the books are interesting.
  • Singular or Plural: some, any, all, most, none (depending on the object of the preposition).
    • Some of the pie is gone.
    • Some of the apples are rotten.
    • All of the water is clean.
    • All of the students are present.

üë™Collective Nouns

  • Rule: Collective nouns (like team, family, group, committee) can be singular or plural depending on whether they are acting as a single unit or as individual members.
    • The team is playing well. (as a unit)
    • The team are arguing about the strategy. (as individuals)
    • The family is large. (as a unit)
    • The family are discussing their plans. (as individuals)

üìèIntervening Phrases

  • Rule: Phrases that come between the subject and verb do not affect subject-verb agreement.
    • The book, along with the CDs, is on the shelf.
    • The students, including the captain, are ready for the game.

üìöTitles of Works

  • Rule: Titles of books, movies, songs, etc., are treated as singular, even if they contain plural words.
    • "The Three Musketeers" is a classic novel.
    • "A Hundred Years of Solitude" is a masterpiece.

üìêUnits of Measurement

  • Rule: Units of measurement (like time, money, distance) are generally treated as singular when they represent a single quantity.
    • Five dollars is too much to pay.
    • Ten miles is a long way to walk.
    • Two hours is not enough time.

üëâ"There is/There are"

  • Rule: The verb agrees with the noun that follows "there is/there are."
    • There is a book on the table.
    • There are several books on the table.

üî¢Numbers

  • Rule: Numbers can be tricky. Context often determines singularity or plurality.
    • Fifty percent of the class is present.
    • Fifty percent of the students are present.
