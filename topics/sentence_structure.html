Simple Sentences

A simple sentence has just one independent clause with a subject and a verb. It expresses a complete thought.

The cat sleeps. | My brother plays soccer. | Sarah loves ice cream.

Compound Sentences

A compound sentence contains two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so).

I like chocolate, but my sister prefers vanilla. | He plays piano, and she dances.

Complex Sentences

A complex sentence has one independent clause and at least one dependent clause. The dependent clause starts with words like because, since, after, although, or when.

When it rains, the streets get wet. | Because he was late, he missed the bus.

Top Tips for Sentence Structure Mastery

Vary Your Sentence Types

Use a mix of simple, compound, and complex sentences to make your writing more interesting. Using only one type can make your writing sound monotonous.

Watch Your Sentence Length

Short sentences pack a punch. Longer sentences allow for more detail. Alternate between them for rhythm in your writing.

Be Careful with Fragments

Sentence fragments don't express a complete thought. They might be missing a subject or verb. Use them for effect in creative writing, but avoid them in formal writing.

Use Parallel Structure

When you list items in a sentence, keep them in the same grammatical form: "I enjoy swimming, hiking, and reading" (all -ing verbs).

Common Sentence Structure Errors

Incorrect

Although it was raining. We went to the park.

Problem: This is a sentence fragment followed by a complete sentence.

Correct

Although it was raining, we went to the park.

Fix: Join the dependent clause with the independent clause.


Incorrect

I love chocolate cake, my sister prefers ice cream.

Problem: This is a comma splice - two independent clauses joined only by a comma.

Correct

I love chocolate cake, and my sister prefers ice cream.

Fix: Add a coordinating conjunction after the comma.
